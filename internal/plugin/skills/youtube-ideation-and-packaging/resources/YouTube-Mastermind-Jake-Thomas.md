# Expert Profile: Jake Thomas (Creator Hooks)

## Background & Credibility

Jake Thomas is a title specialist with extensive marketing experience, initially working at a Facebook Ad Agency before transitioning to YouTube strategy. He's the founder of Creator Hooks, a newsletter dedicated to YouTube title analysis. His channels have collectively generated over 100 million views, and he has personally analysed nearly 1,000 viral YouTube titles. Thomas gained expertise as a YouTube channel manager for a fishing company where he was responsible for dramatically improving their title performance (3-4x revenue increase). He maintains a database of over 400 analysed titles and provides regular breakdowns of viral videos in his newsletter.

## Core Philosophy

Thomas believes that effective YouTube titles create an emotional response by leveraging human psychology through three primary "click-worthy emotions" (curiosity, fear, and desire), with the most powerful titles combining curiosity with either fear or desire. His fundamental approach can be summarised as: "Don't be original - always base titles off previously successful videos."

## Key Frameworks & Models

### Click-Worthy Emotions Framework

Thomas identifies three primary emotions that drive clicks:

- **Curiosity**: Present in 60-61% of viral titles analysed
- **Desire**: Present in 46% of viral titles analysed
- **Fear/Negativity**: Present in 39-40% of viral titles analysed

Most effective titles combine curiosity with either fear or desire. This framework is consistent across his analysis of nearly 1,000 titles over multiple years, suggesting these psychological drivers are timeless.

### Click Triggers System

Thomas has developed a system of 33 specific "click triggers" - tactical approaches to writing titles that provoke emotional responses and drive clicks. These include:

1. **Opening a loop**: Starting a story without finishing it to build curiosity
2. **Revealing a secret**: Making viewers feel they're getting insider information
3. **Being counterintuitive**: Presenting information that contradicts expectations
4. **Timeliness/trend-jacking**: Leveraging current events or trends (most powerful but ephemeral)
5. **Authority references**: Using credible figures or experts to build trust
6. **Regrets/mistakes**: Leveraging fear through cautionary content
7. **Labels**: Using targeted descriptive terms that resonate with specific audiences
8. **Year markers**: Adding the current year to signal relevance and timeliness
9. **Contrast**: Creating curiosity through opposing concepts
10. **Objections**: Addressing common barriers in the title itself

### The Dream 10 & Model 10 Research Framework

Thomas recommends creating two lists to guide content strategy:

- **Dream 10**: Competitors discussing the same topics as you (topic alignment)
- **Model 10**: Creators with the same content style but different topics (format alignment)

These lists serve as comprehensive research sources that can answer nearly any question about YouTube strategy, from thumbnail design to monetisation approaches, without directly copying competitors.

### Title Testing Methodology

Thomas advocates for structured A/B testing of titles with specific parameters:

- Wait 5+ days before testing to avoid subscriber bias in early data
- Test for 7 days per title variation
- Use tools like TubeBuddy or ThumbnailTest.com
- Evaluate both CTR and watch time metrics
- Use test results as directional indicators of trends rather than absolute rules

## Strategic Approaches

### Content Strategy

Thomas emphasises modelling successful content rather than pursuing originality. His approach:

1. Start with a strong idea (the "pig" that no amount of lipstick can save)
2. Research what's working for channels similar to yours in size (not mega-creators)
3. Look outside your niche for fresh inspiration and frameworks
4. Apply emotional triggers appropriate to your audience and topic
5. Frame concepts in ways that directly address audience desires or fears

### Audience Building

Thomas advocates for understanding audience psychology at a deep level:

- Target beginners when possible (they're "hungriest" and most insecure)
- Address specific audience segments in titles to increase relevance
- Research audience myths and misconceptions to create counterintuitive content
- Frame content around audience's "deepest desires" expressed better than they could themselves
- Establish credibility through demonstrating experience rather than theoretical knowledge

### Analytics & Optimisation

Thomas has a data-driven approach to optimisation:

- Uses "hook score" to identify outlier videos (views compared to channel average)
- Tracks data in structured spreadsheets to identify patterns
- Focuses on CTR and watch time correlation in title testing
- Segments data by traffic source (search vs. browse/recommend) to optimise differently
- Builds comprehensive title databases to identify emotional patterns

### Monetisation

Thomas mentions limited monetisation insights, noting:

- Title optimisation dramatically impacted revenue (3-4x) for his fishing channel
- Strong titles can serve as force multipliers for expensive content investments
- Even "boring" B2B content can see 2-5x view increases through title optimisation

## Distinctive Viewpoints

### Against Direct Competitor Copying

While Thomas advocates modelling success, he strongly advises against directly copying competitors in your niche. Instead, he recommends:

- Getting inspiration from outside your niche
- Taking frameworks from other industries and adapting them
- Looking at competitive niches like finance, entrepreneurship, fitness
- Studying content with similar formats but different topics

### Title Length Strategy

Thomas believes shorter titles are generally better:

- Recommends 45-55 characters maximum for most titles
- Advises against using "and" in titles as it dilutes focus
- Notes that even two-word titles ("Clear Ketchup") can be effective if they build curiosity
- Suggests keeping titles focused on a single idea rather than multiple concepts

### Search vs. Browse Optimisation Differences

Thomas makes important distinctions between optimising for different traffic sources:

- **Browse/Homepage**: Titles should be shorter, simpler, and "get out of the way"
- **Search**: Titles can be longer and more keyword-focused as users are actively seeking information
- **Education channels**: Titles matter more than thumbnails
- **Entertainment channels**: Thumbnails matter more than titles

### Thumbnail-Title Relationship

Thomas believes thumbnails and titles should complement rather than repeat each other:

- Avoid duplicating the same concept in both
- Use thumbnails to create curiosity and titles to explain or elaborate
- For educational content, titles are more important
- For entertainment content, thumbnails are more important

### On Chapter Markers

Contrary to many creators, Thomas doesn't use chapter markers for his videos:

- Believes they encourage selective viewing rather than complete watch-through
- Prefers to "suck viewers in" to watch the entire content
- Acknowledges this may not provide optimal user experience

## Actionable Tactics

### Title Research Methods

1. Create "Dream 10" and "Model 10" lists for reference
2. Search for successful titles in adjacent niches, not just your own
3. Look for patterns in high-performing outlier videos
4. Study titles from competitive niches (finance, entrepreneurship, fitness)
5. Research how competitors phrase audience desires and fears
6. Use ChatGPT to identify audience myths, fears, and objections
7. Search your proposed title to see if similar ones have performed well historically

### Title Testing Protocol

1. Wait 5+ days after publishing before testing titles
2. Use TubeBuddy or ThumbnailTest.com for structured testing
3. Set tests to run for 7 days per variation
4. Analyse both CTR and average view duration
5. Look for patterns across multiple tests rather than single-test outcomes
6. Use test data as a "compass" for directional insights

### Title Optimisation Tactics

1. For search traffic: Place keywords at the beginning, emotional hooks at the end
2. Add the current year to titles (especially effective November-January)
3. Target beginners when possible ("for beginners", "beginner's guide")
4. Use contrast to create curiosity (e.g., "dumb product" vs "million dollars")
5. Remove the word "and" from titles to maintain focus
6. Keep titles under 55 characters to avoid truncation
7. Avoid copying mega-creators whose name recognition carries their content
8. For guest interviews: Consider using a descriptive label rather than just a name

### Email Subject Line Insights (Applicable to Titles)

1. Single-subject lines outperform multi-topic ones (35-40% vs. 45-55% open rates)
2. Modelling viral video titles for email subjects can dramatically increase open rates
3. Using labels instead of technical terms can double engagement (e.g., "teenage golden retrievers" vs. "5-month-old golden retrievers")
4. Apply the same emotional triggers (curiosity, fear, desire) to email subjects

## 33 Click Triggers for Irresistible YouTube Titles

### Description

Jake Thomas identifies 33 click triggers that repeatedly appear in viral YouTube titles. These are psychological mechanisms that spark emotions - primarily curiosity, fear, or desire - and compel users to click. Each trigger taps into specific patterns of human behaviour and attention.

### Steps / Components (Expanded List of Click Triggers)

1. **Curiosity**
   - Most common and powerful trigger.
   - Acts like a "mental mosquito bite" - viewers _need_ to resolve the unknown.
   - **Examples of how to build it:**
     - Open a loop (e.g., start a story without finishing it)
     - Reveal a secret (e.g., "You won't believe…")
     - Ask a question (e.g., "What happens if you eat only meat for a month?")
     - Be extreme or unique ("The _worst_ punishments in human history")
     - Use contrast ("$1 vs $1M vacation")
     - Be counterintuitive ("How I slept 4 hours a day and felt better")
     - Predict the future ("What YouTube will look like in 2030")
     - Be weird ("The strange trick that got me a million views")

2. **Fear**
   - Hardwired into humans; primal survival instinct.
   - Fear of loss, failure, danger.
   - "News stations have used this forever."
   - **Ways to apply:**
     - Use threatening language ("Why your channel might get deleted…")
     - Highlight danger or loss ("The YouTube setting that's killing your views")

3. **Negativity**
   - Closely linked to fear; includes multiple subtypes:
   - **Sub-triggers:**
     - **Drama** - Viewers are drawn to conflict.
     - **Warnings** - "Stop doing this if you want to grow."
     - **Pain points** - "No one is watching your videos? Do this."
     - **Regret** - "My biggest regret after 100 videos."
     - **Mistakes** - "5 mistakes killing your channel."
     - **FOMO** - "What you're missing in your uploads."
     - Framed either strongly ("You will regret this") or personally ("I regret doing this").

4. **List**
   - Structured, digestible, and easy to promise value.
   - Examples: "5 steps to start a YouTube channel", "Top 10 tools for creators".

5. **Timeliness**
   - Uses urgency and relevance.
   - Adds the current year (e.g., "in 2024") to capture attention.
   - Best used from November-March, during transition into a new year.
   - Also includes trendjacking and reacting to current events.

6. **Authority**
   - Name-drop a person or role with perceived expertise or inside knowledge.
   - Example: "Ex-Taco Bell employee reveals the truth…"

7. **Time Frame**
   - Makes the value feel immediate and measurable.
   - Examples: "45 years of business knowledge in 45 minutes"
   - Even product names like "5-Hour Energy" follow this formula.

8. **Call Out a Specific Audience**
   - Makes the viewer feel it was made "just for them".
   - Example: "Best investments for men in their 20s"

9. **Beginner Framing**
   - Signals accessibility to new or intimidated viewers.
   - Example: "YouTube tips for beginners"

10. **Epic**
    - Feels historical, monumental, or grand.
    - Example: "Worst punishments in _human history_"

11. **Refute an Objection**
    - Directly removes a viewer's inner resistance.
    - Example: "How to start a channel without being on camera"

12. **Daily Tips**
    - Indicates habit and consistency.
    - Example: "Do this every day to grow your channel"

13. **X vs Y (Comparison)**
    - Highlights a decision or contrast.
    - Example: "YouTube vs TikTok - Which is better in 2024?"

14. **Constraints**
    - Show achievement _despite_ limitations.
    - Example: "I started a channel without showing my face"

15. **Confirm Suspicions**
    - Validates a viewer's belief or hunch.
    - Example: "Ex-employee confirms Taco Bell meat isn't what you think"
    - "If your viewer says, 'I _knew_ it', you win."

16. **Desire**
    - Speaks to deep wants or ambitions.
    - Example: "How to grow fast on YouTube in 2024"
    - Pairs well with time frame, list, or how-to structure.

17. **Urgency** _(Less common but still used)_
    - Rarely used well on YouTube due to evergreen nature of content.
    - Might appear in formats like "Before it's too late" or "Act fast"

### Key Examples

- "My biggest regret in starting a YouTube channel" — Curiosity + Regret
- "How I slept 4 hours a day and felt better" — Curiosity + Counterintuitive
- "Ex-Taco Bell employee reveals the truth" — Authority + Confirm Suspicions
- "5 steps to start a YouTube channel" — List + Beginner
- "Do this every day to grow on YouTube" — Daily Tip + Desire
- "$1 yacht vs $1M yacht" — Contrast + Epic

### Definitions

- **Click Trigger**: A specific psychological or emotional technique embedded in a title to drive attention and compel action.
- **Curiosity**: A mental itch that needs to be scratched; viewer feels compelled to click to satisfy uncertainty.
- **Fear**: Emotion rooted in avoiding danger or harm; evokes immediate attention.
- **Desire**: Appeal to dreams, success, or ideal future outcomes.
- **Regret**: A powerful emotional reflection on missed opportunity or failure.
- **Confirming Suspicions**: Titles that reinforce what viewers already believe but lack proof of.

### Principles / Best Practices

- Master a few triggers instead of trying all 33.
- Choose triggers based on your audience's psychology (e.g., what they fear, desire, or are curious about).
- Mix title and thumbnail click triggers for a "1-2 punch" effect.
- Always deliver on the promise of the title to maintain trust and avoid misleading viewers.
- Use simple phrasing that even a six-year-old could understand.
- Test titles based on emotional category: curiosity vs fear vs desire.

### Phrases / Templates

- "How I did [result] without [expected requirement]"
- "What happens if you [unexpected action] for 30 days?"
- "The [weird/secret] reason I [succeeded/failed]"
- "Top 5 [tools/habits/mistakes] in 2024"
- "$1 vs $1M [product/experience]"
- "Don't do this if you want to [achieve goal]"
- "Do this every day to [achieve goal]"
- "What [celebrity/expert] says about [topic]"

## Click-Worthy Emotions Framework for Titles

### Description

This framework identifies the core emotional triggers that make people click on YouTube videos. The best titles create an emotional response, primarily by using three click-worthy emotions. These emotions are often combined - especially curiosity with either fear or desire - to increase engagement. The framework also guides how these emotions can be strategically used across titles, thumbnails, and subject lines, particularly in educational versus entertainment content.

### Steps / Components

1. **Curiosity**: The primary emotional trigger used to provoke interest and compel clicks.
2. **Fear**: Creates urgency or warns of negative consequences. Often used with curiosity.
3. **Desire**: Tied to curiosity to amplify interest in potential benefits or positive outcomes.
4. **Combination Strategy**: Most effective titles combine curiosity with either fear or desire.

### Key Examples

- "Seven things plant experts do that you probably don't" — curiosity + fear (FOMO).
- "How a pro YouTuber actually makes thumbnails" — desire + curiosity + authority.
- "Why teenage golden retrievers were the worst" — fear (negativity) + labelling + curiosity.
- "Leave the United States before 2030" — fear + curiosity.
- "10 Mistakes to Buying Land" with thumbnail text "Don't do this" — fear + curiosity.
- "Clear Ketchup" — extreme curiosity from counterintuitive phrasing.
- "How to get hot without stopping your ice cream habit" — desire + objection handling.
- "How to get stronger without lifting weights" — desire + objection handling.
- "This new approach to YouTube will change your channel forever" — desire + curiosity + timeliness.
- "How this dumb product made a million dollars" — curiosity through contrast (artful wording).

### Definitions

- **Click-Worthy Emotions**: Curiosity, fear, and desire — emotions that trigger someone to click.
- **Opening a loop**: Starting a story but not finishing it, used to build curiosity.
- **FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)**: A form of fear used to spark urgency or insecurity.
- **Authority**: Referencing professionals or experts to establish trust and boost interest.
- **Labelling**: Framing the subject with a descriptive or emotional term (e.g., "teenage" retrievers).
- **Click Trigger**: A specific emotional or psychological prompt that increases likelihood of clicking.
- **Timeliness**: A powerful click trigger based on current events or time-sensitive context (also called trend jacking or newsjacking).
- **Objection Handling**: Anticipating resistance and addressing it directly in the title ("without doing X").

### Principles / Best Practices

- Use curiosity, fear, or desire to provoke an emotional response.
- Combine curiosity with fear or desire for stronger performance.
- Avoid bland, emotionless statements in titles.
- Study titles across diverse industries to identify emotional patterns.
- Call out specific audiences (e.g., beginners) to create stronger resonance.
- Titles are more important for educational content; thumbnails are more important for entertainment.
- Use emotional hooks like "regret" in first-person to reduce clickbait ickiness ("I regret…" vs. "You will regret…").
- Don't try to be original when starting - model what's already working.
- Keep titles under ~55 characters to avoid truncation and confusion.
- Avoid using "and" in titles - it dilutes focus and emotional impact.
- If testing titles, wait at least five days post-publish for better CTR data.
- Use one subject or emotion per title to maintain clarity and power.
- Match title tone with your content's audience (i.e., advanced vs. beginner).
