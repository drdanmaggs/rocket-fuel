# Expert Profile: Paddy Galloway

## Background & Credibility

Paddy Galloway is described as "the most prolific YouTube strategist in the world." Over the past decade, he has worked with thousands of channels (including Mr. Beast and Red Bull) and generated over 10 billion views. His expertise spans multiple niches, from large entertainment channels to specialised content like permaculture and woodworking.

## Core Philosophy

Galloway firmly believes any niche can achieve viral success on YouTube when properly packaged and positioned. He challenges the limiting belief that certain topics are "too small" or "too boring" to attract significant viewership.

## Key Frameworks & Models

### Total Addressable Market (TAM)

- **Definition**: The potential audience size for a YouTube channel within its niche
- **How to Calculate**:
  - Search for your niche on YouTube
  - Filter by most viewed videos
  - Analyse top-performing videos in your space
  - Use these metrics as benchmarks for your potential
- **Application**: Compare your current performance against top channels in your niche to determine growth potential (e.g., if top channels get 1M views and you're getting 100K, you're only at 10% of your potential)
- **Case Example**: Noah Kagan shifting from niche business tool topics to broader topics like "asking millionaires for advice" to expand his TAM

## Strategic Approaches

### Content Strategy

- **Niche Expansion**: Start with a narrower focus on core audience, then strategically expand outward
- **Breadth vs. Depth**: Think of niches as expanding rather than limiting; look for broader applications of niche expertise
- **Topic Selection**: Choose topics that can bridge specialised knowledge with general interest
- **Value Equation**: Packaging and presentation improvements yield non-linear returns (a 20% better title could mean 100x more views)
- **Balance**: Perfectionism must be balanced with consistent output; set time limits on overthinking

### Audience Building

- **Audience Segments**: Design content to simultaneously satisfy core fans, casual viewers, and new audiences
- **Expansion Approach**: Use "monkey bars" technique to gradually expand to adjacent interests with overlapping audience segments
- **Views Maximisation**: Most beneficial when primary revenue is from AdSense and sponsorships, especially below full-time income level
- **Targeted Approach**: More appropriate when selling specific products/courses to niche audiences

### Analytics & Optimisation

- **Key Metrics to Watch**:
  - Demographics (different countries, age groups)
  - Device type (TV, mobile, desktop)
  - New vs. returning viewers ratio
  - Traffic sources
  - First 24-hour click-through rate (strong predictor of success)
- **Performance Analysis**: When a video outperforms expectations, analyse these factors to understand why
- **Iterative Improvement**: Small, continuous improvements in packaging create compounding effects due to YouTube's non-linear nature

### Pre-Production & Packaging

- **Resource Allocation**: Top creators invest significantly more time in pre-production compared to beginners
- **ROI Perspective**: Small improvements in packaging yield exponential returns
- **Decision Framework**: YouTube success requires strategic decision-making about which opportunities to pursue
- **Testing Protocol**: Get feedback on multiple thumbnail and title options before publishing

## Distinctive Viewpoints

### "There is no meta"

- Strongly contests the idea that there's a single formula for YouTube success
- Argues YouTube is too diverse for any one approach to dominate
- Notes that even during peak "Mr. Beast-ification," that style only accounted for 5-10% of trending videos
- Warns creators not to get caught up in declared "new directions" or trends presented as universal

### Challenge to "My niche is too small" mindset

- Views this as an excuse rather than a legitimate limitation
- Shows how seemingly "boring" niches (horticulture, golf, knitting) can achieve millions of views
- Emphasises that most creators blaming their niche are significantly underperforming relative to the niche's potential

### Views on the nonlinear nature of YouTube success

- Stresses that small improvements don't yield proportional results, but exponential ones
- A 20% better title might result in 2-100x more views, not just 20% more views
- This nonlinearity justifies significant time investment in packaging details

### Balanced approach to perfectionism

- Advocates setting time limits on overthinking (e.g., spend an hour on title, not a week)
- Accepts that every video leaves some views on the table; perfection is impossible
- Recognises that timeline matters as much as perfection; publishing consistently trumps perfect execution

## Actionable Tactics

### Thumbnail Creation

- **Three Element Rule**: No more than three focal points in a thumbnail
- **Glance Test**: Thumbnail should be processable in milliseconds
- **Empty Space**: Utilise empty space rather than filling every part of the thumbnail
- **Strategic Exaggeration**: Slight exaggeration without misleading (e.g., making expensive items look shinier)
- **Simplicity Over Detail**: Beginners often fill thumbnails with too much information
- **Cultural Adaptation**: Consider geographic differences (e.g., Asian markets often prefer more text)

### Title Optimisation

- Invest significant time in title exploration (but with time limits)
- Focus on clarity and immediate understanding
- Test multiple options before committing
- Consider how title and thumbnail work together as a package
- Prioritise titles that appeal across core, casual, and new viewer segments

### Video Idea Evaluation

- **Specific Questions to Ask**:
  - "Does this appeal to core, casual and new viewers?"
  - "What makes this unique or novel?"
  - "Can I convey this easily in title & thumbnail?"
  - "Do others get excited when I mention this idea?"
  - "Is this feasible to create with our resources?"
  - "Am I genuinely excited to make this?"

### Analysing Successful Videos

- **Primary Questions**:
  - What made this unique?
  - What about the packaging made it special?
  - Why did this go viral when similar videos didn't?
- **Data Points to Check**:
  - Demographics variations
  - Device type distribution
  - First 24-hour click-through rate
  - Traffic sources
  - New vs. returning viewer ratio

## CCN Framework (Core, Casual, New)

### Description

A framework for evaluating the potential reach and appeal of YouTube video ideas by ensuring they engage three categories of viewers: core, casual, and new. The goal is to expand viewership while maintaining relevance to a channel's existing audience.

### Steps / Components

1. **Core** - These are the most dedicated fans or viewers within your niche. The video should provide value to them.
2. **Casual** - Viewers who dip in and out of your niche, or who have some level of interest but aren't deeply invested. The video should still interest this broader segment.
3. **New** - People who are outside the niche or unfamiliar with your content entirely. The video should have an angle, hook, or packaging that can catch their attention and encourage a click.

### Key Examples

- **Permaculture Channel (Andrew Millison)**:
  - Niche: Horticulture / Permaculture
  - Viral Video: _"How the UN is Holding Back the Sahara Desert"_ - 12 million views in 4 months.
  - Analysis: While his niche is highly specific, the video appealed to:
    - Core (permaculture enthusiasts)
    - Casual (people interested in sustainability or development)
    - New (general interest due to the hook involving the UN and Sahara Desert)

- **Woodworking Channel (731 Woodworks)**:
  - Viral formats included:
    - _"99% of Woodworkers Get This Wrong"_
    - _"This Tool is Now Illegal in Seven and a Half States"_
  - Analysis: These titles appeal to:
    - Core (experienced woodworkers)
    - Casual (DIY hobbyists)
    - New (people intrigued by controversy or legality angle)

- **Noah Kagan**:
  - Initially created narrow business content (e.g., "How to use Slack").
  - Shifted to broader ideas like "Asking millionaires for advice".
  - Analysis: Broader appeal brought in new and casual viewers while still retaining his entrepreneurial core audience.

### Definitions

- **CCN**: A framework for identifying whether a video idea appeals to:
  - **Core** - deeply invested audience in the niche
  - **Casual** - loosely connected audience
  - **New** - those outside the niche but potentially interested through broader themes

### Principles / Best Practices

- Ask of every video idea: "Can a core, casual, and new viewer all click this video and enjoy it?"
- Avoid focusing too narrowly on just the core audience; it limits growth.
- Avoid going too broad and losing connection with your core audience.
- Expand niche appeal by identifying "upstream" interests - larger categories your topic fits within.
- Use existing top-performing videos in your niche to estimate TAM (Total Addressable Market) and spot viewership benchmarks.
- Apply the CCN framework in tandem with novelty and title/thumbnail clarity for viral potential.

### Phrases / Templates

- "What are the traits of a viral video within a niche? The first thing on that list… is what I call CCN: core, casual, new."
- "Can a core, casual, and new viewer all click this video and enjoy it?"
- "They get too caught up in just the core… and they forget about the casual and the new viewer."
- "If you're just going after the new viewer… you're not bringing along your core with you."

## The Idea Funnel Process

### Description

A structured process for sourcing, filtering, and developing video ideas in a way that maximises the chance of success. It ensures ideas are viable, aligned with audience appeal (using CCN), and fully fleshed out before production begins.

### Steps / Components

#### Stage 1: Idea Sources

1. **Internal Ideas** - Analyse what has previously worked on your own channel. This includes identifying top-performing videos and thinking of ways to expand, remix, or iterate on them.
2. **External Ideas** - Look at high-performing videos from other channels within your niche or similar niches. Use tools like YouTube search filters or platforms like "One of Ten" to identify outlier content.
3. **Innovation (Blue Ocean Concepts)** - Develop entirely new, original formats or angles that aren't widely seen yet in your niche. This could include cross-niche concepts or trends borrowed from other verticals.

> "I was like to say there's three main sources of ideas: internal ideas, external ideas, and then innovation."

#### Stage 2: Filtering Process

1. **Does it have CCN fit?** - Will the idea resonate with Core, Casual, and New audiences? Apply the CCN framework to each idea.
   > "Every idea... ask: Can a core, casual, and new viewer all click this video and enjoy it?"
2. **Is it feasible to create?** - Consider production limitations, access to resources, budget, and time. Eliminate ideas that are unrealistic.
   > "Let's say we're brainstorming for a wildlife channel: 'I swam covered in blood with 10 great white sharks' - yes, that might go viral, but it might also kill the creator."
3. **Can you create a compelling title and thumbnail?** - If you can't express the idea clearly and intriguingly in a title and thumbnail, it's not a strong idea.
   > "If you can't title or thumbnail it, you don't have a great idea."
4. **Are you genuinely excited to make it?** - Assess the creative motivation. Without excitement or interest, execution quality and consistency may suffer.
   > "If the creator doesn't like doing it, they're going to burn out. That hurts long term."

#### Stage 3: Development Process

1. **Create One-Page Idea Development for Top 10 Ideas**
   - For each idea that passes the filters, develop a one-pager that includes:
     - A rough sketch of the thumbnail
     - A working title
     - A "Why" section (1-3 reasons the idea will work)
     - A logline that briefly outlines the structure or concept of the video

   > "On a one-pager I like to have: rough thumbnail sketch, rough title, a Y Square with 1-3 points why the video will work, and the logline."

2. **Get Feedback from Others**
   - Share your idea pages with teammates, other creators, or even newer team members for fresh perspectives.
   - Avoid isolation and over-reliance on your own judgment.

   > "Even just asking a random new editor on the team - 'what do you think of this idea?' - helps avoid isolation."

3. **Develop Final 3-4 Priority Videos**
   - From the top 10, use the feedback and development to narrow down the most viable and exciting 3-4 ideas.
   - These are the videos you prioritise for production.

### Key Examples

- **Example of Unfeasible Idea**: Swimming covered in blood with sharks. Would likely go viral but is not safe or practical.
- **Example of External Inspiration**: Borrowing viral formats from unrelated niches, e.g., "I tested 100 years of kids' toys" → adapted into "I tested 100 years of productivity hacks."

### Definitions

- **One-Page Idea Development**: A document used to fully explore a video idea before production. Includes thumbnail sketch, proposed title, rationale ("Why" section), and a logline describing the video content.

### Principles / Best Practices

- Don't move to production until you've passed ideas through all filters.
- Use a systematic approach to avoid wasting time and creative energy on poor ideas.
- Prioritise both creative excitement and strategic feasibility.
- Keep idea generation ongoing by pulling from internal, external, and innovative sources.
- Validate with others to avoid echo chambers or overreliance on personal taste.

### Phrases / Templates

- "There's three main sources of ideas: internal ideas, external ideas, and then innovation."
- "Can a core, casual, and new viewer all click this video and enjoy it?"
- "You don't have a great idea if you can't title or thumbnail it."
- "Even just asking a new editor: what do you think of this idea?"

## Phases of YouTube Channel Growth

### Description

A three-phase framework that outlines the stages a YouTube channel typically goes through on its path from beginner to growth and optimisation. Each phase emphasises different goals and skills, guiding creators from foundational execution to strategic refinement.

### Steps / Components

#### Phase 1: Establishment

1. **Purpose**: Learn the basic skills and get into the habit of publishing consistently.

2. **Key Focus Areas**:
   - Learn how to edit videos
   - Learn how to design thumbnails
   - Develop storytelling and scripting fundamentals
   - Understand basic camera, lighting, and audio

3. **Output Goal**: Publish consistently (ideally 1-2 videos per week)

4. **Mindset**: Don't overthink strategy. Focus on execution and reps.

   > "We're building those fundamental skills. We're making videos... maybe one or two videos every single week."

5. **Milestone for Transition**:
   - You've published 30-50 videos.
   - You've developed basic production competency.
   - You're posting consistently.

#### Phase 2: Improvement

1. **Purpose**: Begin layering in strategy and structured thinking.

2. **Key Focus Areas**:
   - Study YouTube Studio data (e.g., retention curves, traffic sources)
   - Prioritise brainstorming and idea selection
   - Improve packaging (title and thumbnail)
   - Apply frameworks like CCN and The Idea Funnel

3. **Practice Structured Ideation**:
   - Schedule weekly brainstorming sessions
   - Use idea funnels to vet and prioritise video concepts

   > "Now I need to keep improving upon that establishment... start thinking about strategy and idea generation more deeply."

4. **Milestone for Transition**:
   - You're making well-packaged videos.
   - You've identified what works on your channel.
   - You've started building repeatable processes.

#### Phase 3: Optimisation

1. **Purpose**: Maximise growth, efficiency, and performance at scale.

2. **Key Focus Areas**:
   - Refine what's already working using data
   - Improve creative decision-making
   - Use tools and feedback loops to test and iterate packaging
   - Expand your team or processes to scale

   > "You end up optimising and tweaking to get to that Final Destination you want to be."

3. **Strategic Layering**:
   - Implement structured feedback systems
   - Use data (CTR, viewer demographics, device type) to guide decisions
   - Test packaging variations (title and thumbnail)

### Key Examples

- **Beginner Focus**: "Just publish. Don't get stuck overthinking every decision. Focus on reps."
- **Improvement Phase**: Begin using The Idea Funnel, CCN, and packaging iteration to improve ideas before production.
- **Optimisation Phase**: Red Bull video case study - "World's Fastest Camera Drone vs F1 Car" - small differences in title could mean 10M+ difference in views due to nonlinear returns.

### Definitions

- **Establishment Phase**: A stage of building basic production skills and publishing consistently to learn through doing.
- **Improvement Phase**: A stage of learning strategy and applying structured methods to video ideation and packaging.
- **Optimisation Phase**: A stage of refining and scaling what works using advanced tactics and data insights.

### Principles / Best Practices

- Don't start with optimisation - start by learning the craft.
- You can't scale something that doesn't work yet.
- Develop foundational skills before diving into advanced tactics.
- Strategy should follow experience, not precede it.
- Think of growth as a staircase: build, improve, then optimise.

### Phrases / Templates

- "You're not really good at the start, so you need to have that repetition over time."
- "Once you have 30-50 videos and have learned the skills... now we have a foundation we can put strategy on top."
- "It becomes a game of decision-making - what ideas do we pursue, and how do we package them?"

## Traits of Viral Videos Framework

### Description

A checklist-style framework used to evaluate whether a video idea has the core characteristics that commonly lead to virality on YouTube. It helps creators assess whether an idea has mass appeal, uniqueness, clarity, and resonance.

### Steps / Components

1. **CCN Fit (Core, Casual, New)**
   - The idea should be appealing to:
     - **Core** viewers: loyal fans of the niche.
     - **Casual** viewers: those loosely interested or occasionally engaged.
     - **New** viewers: people unfamiliar with the niche but curious if the topic is interesting enough.

   > "Core casual new - that just changes how you approach YouTube."

2. **Unique Novelty Value**
   - The video should feel fresh, like it brings something new to the table.
   - Ask: _"What is unique about this idea?"_
   - Examples:
     - New information
     - A twist on a familiar concept
     - A surprising or counterintuitive angle

   > "Something about it that makes this video just feel like it's its own thing, its own island."

3. **Easy to Convey in Title and Thumbnail**
   - The core idea must be immediately understandable through simple and compelling packaging.
   - If you can't title or thumbnail it easily, it's not a great idea.

   > "A great idea has to be easy to convey in a title and thumbnail."

4. **Excitement and Gut Reaction from Others**
   - When shared with peers, teammates, or a creative council, the idea should spark genuine interest or excitement.
   - Ask for informal reactions to validate emotional resonance.

   > "Do they get excited? Do they genuinely feel like 'oh, that's interesting'?"

### Key Examples

- **Andrew Millison's 12M View Video**:
  - _"How the UN is Holding Back the Sahara Desert"_
  - Novel framing, wide appeal, CCN-aligned, and clickable.
- **731 Woodworks**:
  - _"99% of Woodworkers Get This Wrong"_, _"This Tool is Now Illegal in Seven and a Half States"_
  - Clear titles, broad intrigue, CCN fit, and novelty for the niche.

### Definitions

- **Novelty Value**: The perceived uniqueness or freshness of an idea that makes it stand out among similar content.
- **CCN Fit**: A measurement of how well a video idea appeals to Core, Casual, and New viewers.

### Principles / Best Practices

- Virality is rarely accidental - it often comes from careful alignment of these traits.
- Don't mistake technical quality (e.g., new camera or colour grading) for uniqueness - focus on the idea.
- Run every idea through this four-part filter before production.
- Use feedback loops (friends, team, community) to test resonance before filming.

### Phrases / Templates

- "Even for my own channel, I'd say: the world's biggest creator should be able to watch this and my mother should be able to watch this."
- "You don't have a great idea if you can't title or thumbnail it."
- "What is unique? What makes this stand out?"
- "Do they get excited? Do they genuinely feel like 'oh, that's interesting'?"
